Let S(α,n)=∑nk=1⌊αk⌋S(α,n)=∑k=1n⌊αk⌋ for αα some irrationnal positive number.

if α≥2α≥2 we let β=α−1β=α−1 and you get
S(α,n)=S(β,n)+∑nk=1k=S(β,n)+n(n+1)/2S(α,n)=S(β,n)+∑k=1nk=S(β,n)+n(n+1)/2

if 1<α<21<α<2, there is a theorem that says if ββ satisfies α−1+β−1=1α−1+β−1=1, then the sequences ⌊αn⌋⌊αn⌋ and ⌊βn⌋⌊βn⌋ for n≥1n≥1 partition NN (not counting 00)

Therefore, letting m=⌊αn⌋m=⌊αn⌋, S(α,n)+S(β,⌊m/β⌋)=∑mk=1k=m(m+1)/2S(α,n)+S(β,⌊m/β⌋)=∑k=1mk=m(m+1)/2
Also, ⌊m/β⌋=m−⌈m/α⌉=m−n=⌊(α−1)n⌋⌊m/β⌋=m−⌈m/α⌉=m−n=⌊(α−1)n⌋.

Then, letting n′=⌊(α−1)n⌋n′=⌊(α−1)n⌋ you have
S(α,n)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(β,n′)S(α,n)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(β,n′)

So those two formulas give you a very fast way to compute SS if you can compute n′=⌊(α−1)n⌋n′=⌊(α−1)n⌋

In your case, α=2–√α=2, so you begin in the second case where you get β=2+2–√β=2+2. Since the sequence of ααs you get is periodic, you can get a recurrence formula :

Let n′=⌊(2–√−1)n⌋n′=⌊(2−1)n⌋,

S(2–√,n)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(2+2–√,n′)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(2–√,n′)−n′(n′+1)=nn′+n(n+1)/2−n′(n′+1)/2−S(2–√,n′)S(2,n)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(2+2,n′)=(n+n′)(n+n′+1)/2−S(2,n′)−n′(n′+1)=nn′+n(n+1)/2−n′(n′+1)/2−S(2,n′)

For example this tells you that S(2–√,5)=22−S(2–√,2)=22−3+S(2–√,0)=19S(2,5)=22−S(2,2)=22−3+S(2,0)=19.

Since at each step nn is approximately multiplied by 2–√−12−1, the arguments decrease exponentially. For n=10100n=10100 you need approximately ⌈100log10/log(2–√−1)⌉=262⌈100log⁡10/log⁡(2−1)⌉=262 steps to complete the recursion. This is basically equivalent to computing the powers of (2–√−1)(2−1) with enough precision and should be doable quickly on any computer.

It is clear that, because the sequence {<n2–√>}{<n2>}(the fractional part) is equidistributed over the interval [0,1)[0,1), we have

∑n=1N⌊n2–√⌋=N(N+1)2–√2−∑n=1N<n2–√>(1)(1)∑n=1N⌊n2⌋=N(N+1)22−∑n=1N<n2>
and for the latter sum,
1N∑n=1N<n2–√>→12(2)(2)1N∑n=1N<n2>→12
as N→∞N→∞.

In other words, we have

∑n=1N⌊n2–√⌋=N(N+1)2–√2−N2+o(N)(3)(3)∑n=1N⌊n2⌋=N(N+1)22−N2+o(N)
as N→∞N→∞.

So , in average, we have

1N∑n=1N⌊n2–√⌋=(N+1)2–√2−12+o(1)(4)(4)1N∑n=1N⌊n2⌋=(N+1)22−12+o(1)
and in fact the remainder term is smaller than 1/21/2.

So we conclude that 1N∑Nn=1⌊n2–√⌋1N∑n=1N⌊n2⌋(which is not an integer) is very close to the nearest integer to the number N2√+2√−12N2+2−12.

One interesting thing I observed is that we in fact have more nice decay of the error term, that is,

1N∑n=1N⌊n2–√⌋=(N+1)2–√2−12+o(1N),(5)(5)1N∑n=1N⌊n2⌋=(N+1)22−12+o(1N),
so, return to our original problem, we come up with
∑n=1N⌊n2–√⌋=N(N+1)2–√2−N2+o(1)(6)(6)∑n=1N⌊n2⌋=N(N+1)22−N2+o(1)
and in fact the error term is again smaller than 1/2. So the sum is the nearest integer to the number
N(N+1)2–√2−N2=N(N2–√+2–√−1)2(7)(7)N(N+1)22−N2=N(N2+2−1)2
.

But the proof could possibly require more nice approximation than just the equidistribution of the sequence. (And there seems even more faster decay of the error term!!)

++Added))

What always true in the above discussion is (3)(3) or the equivalent form (4)(4). So we can exactly figure out the average value of the Beatty sequence of 2–√2, that is, the division of (1)(1) by NN.

However, for the exact computation of the value of the sum (1)(1), we need more precise approximation on the error term like (5)(5) or (6)(6). Unfortunately, (5)(5) is not true and so is (7)(7).

I think the best we can do is this: For any irrational γγ, let L(γ)=1−|1−2<γ>|L(γ)=1−|1−2<γ>|. Then we have

∣∣∣∣∑n=1N⌊nγ⌋−(N(N+1)γ2−N2)∣∣∣∣≤cL(γ)|∑n=1N⌊nγ⌋−(N(N+1)γ2−N2)|≤cL(γ)
with cc a constant irrelevant to γγ and NN(c=2c=2 would actually work)

This essentially asserts that the randomness of the distribution of the sequence {<nγ>}{<nγ>} in [0,1)[0,1) depends on how close <γ><γ> is to 00 or 11(Note that L(γ)/2L(γ)/2 is the minimum distance from <γ><γ> to 00 and to 11. Of course this is really a naive approximation, need to be adjusted in many ways.
